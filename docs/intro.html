Chapter 1 Introduction

1.1 Introduction to Indian and International Statistical systems.

1.1.1 The Indian Statistical System: An In-Depth Overview

The Indian Statistical System is a vast, complex, and highly structured network responsible for capturing the socio-economic and demographic realities of a nation with over 1.4 billion people. It operates within the broader administrative and federal framework of the country.

1.1.1.1 Historical Evolution

  • Ancient and Medieval Roots: Statistics in India are as old as statecraft itself. The ancient Indian treatise, Arthashastra, explicitly refers to a network of village-level accountants responsible for collecting data on economic output. Similarly, the medieval text Ain-i-Akbari details an exhaustive administrative apparatus used to collect data on farm produce and standardizing weights and measurements.
  • The British Raj (1858–1947): The modern official statistical system began taking shape during colonial rule. However, colonial efforts were primarily driven by self-interest—specifically to track markets for English products and manage revenue. Consequently, trade statistics were highly developed, while data on domestic socio-economic development was neglected. British officials also utilized these administrative statistics as propaganda to demonstrate to the British Parliament how well they were governing India.
  • Post-Independence and the Mahalanobis Era: Following independence in 1947, India recognized the pressing need for a robust database to drive economic planning. A serious effort to revamp the system was led by Prof. P.C. Mahalanobis, who introduced the widespread use of random sampling—a revolutionary concept at the time. Under his guidance, the Directorate of National Sample Survey was established in 1950, followed by the Central Statistical Organization (CSO) in 1951, laying the modern foundation of India’s statistical infrastructure.

Following the death of Prof. P.C. Mahalanobis in 1972—the architect of India’s post-independence statistical infrastructure—the Indian Statistical System entered a period of decline. This “post-Mahalanobis era” was characterized by a lack of investment in computing resources, a growing insularity from the rest of the world, and the declining influence of the Planning Commission. To identify these deep-rooted deficiencies and suggest remedies, the central government appointed the National Statistical Commission led by Dr. C. Rangarajan in 2000.

Key Recommendations and Their Outcomes The Rangarajan Commission made several sweeping recommendations to revamp the system. While some were successfully implemented, others remain incomplete:

  • Establishment of the National Statistical Commission (NSC): The Rangarajan Commission recommended creating a permanent, statutory apex body that would be independent of the government and directly responsible to Parliament.

    • Outcome: The government officially constituted the NSC on July 12, 2006, to serve as the nodal and empowered body for all core statistical activities. However, the NSC was never granted the statutory status that the Rangarajan Commission originally recommended.

  • The Collection of Statistics Act, 2008: The Commission recommended reforming the outdated Collection of Statistics Act of 1953 to arm statistical officers with greater powers.

    • Outcome: The new Collection of Statistics Act was enacted in 2008, providing comprehensive legal support to collect various socio-economic and industrial data. Despite this, critics note that the new Act still has limited powers.

  • Professional Leadership (Chief Statistician of India): The Commission recommended that the Secretary of the Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI) should be a professional statistician.

    • Outcome: This was successfully implemented. The post of Chief Statistician of India was created to head the National Statistical Office, and this individual also serves as the Secretary to the Government of India in MoSPI and as the Secretary of the NSC.

  • Data Warehousing and Census Modifications: The Commission recommended transforming the Computer Centre division into a comprehensive data warehouse for official statistics, and modifying the Census Act to allow economic censuses to be conducted during the population house-listing phase.

    • Outcome: The data warehouse remains a goal despite several attempts, and the modification to the Census Act was not implemented.

Structure and Mandate of the NSC Today As established in 2006, the NSC is tasked with evolving policies, priorities, and standards in statistical matters, and ensuring statistical coordination among different agencies. It consists of a part-time Chairperson and four part-time Members, each bringing specialization from distinct fields: economic statistics, social and environment statistics, censuses and surveys, and national accounts/state statistical systems. The Secretary of the Planning Commission also serves as an ex-officio member.

1.1.1.2 Constitutional and Legal Framework

The Indian Constitution dictates how statistical responsibilities are shared. In the Seventh Schedule, the subject of ‘Statistics’ is covered in both the Union List (Entry 94) and the Concurrent List (Entry 45). Because it is on the Concurrent List, both the Central and State Governments have the authority to operate and collect data in this domain.

Critical legislative backing for the system includes:

  • The Census Act, 1948: Empowers the collection of demographic data.
  • The Registration of Births and Deaths Act, 1969: Mandates the Civil Registration System.
  • The Collection of Statistics Act, 2008: Arms statistical officers with the power to collect a wide array of socio-economic and industrial data from enterprises and citizens.

1.1.1.3 Structural Organization: Decentralization

The most defining feature of the Indian Statistical System is that it is laterally and vertically decentralized.

  • Lateral Decentralization (Central Level): At the national level, data collection is divided among various line Ministries and Departments based on their administrative subjects (e.g., the Ministry of Agriculture collects crop data; the Ministry of Commerce collects trade data).
  • Vertical Decentralization (Centre and States): Authority is shared between the Union Government and the States/Union Territories. State statistical organizations collect and generate data for sectors under their purview, and the Centre acts as the coordinating agency to present this data on an all-India basis.

1.1.1.4 Key Institutions at the Centre and State

1. Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI): MoSPI is the nodal agency for the planned development of the statistical system. It oversees the National Statistical Office (NSO), which was formed in May 2019 by merging the Central Statistics Office (CSO) and the National Sample Survey Office (NSSO) to streamline operations.

  • Central Statistics Office (CSO): Responsible for macroeconomic data. Its major activities include compiling National Income Accounting (GDP), processing the Annual Survey of Industries (ASI), conducting Economic Censuses, compiling the Index of Industrial Production (IIP), and setting statistical standards.
  • National Sample Survey Office (NSSO): India’s premier agency for conducting large-scale, nationwide sample surveys. It collects primary data on socio-economic subjects such as employment, consumer expenditure, health, and agriculture.

2. State Directorates of Economics and Statistics (DES): At the apex of the state level is the DES. They coordinate all statistical activities within the state, maintaining uniformity with Central standards. They compute the State Domestic Product (SDP), compile local price indices, and participate in national surveys on a matching-sample basis. The DES operates through District Statistical Offices, extending all the way down to block and village levels.

1.1.2 The International Statistical System: An In-Depth Overview

Because statistics are essential for sustainable economic, environmental, and social development, the international community maintains a highly coordinated system to ensure data comparability, share methodologies, and provide technical assistance globally.

1.1.2.1 The United Nations Statistical Division (UNSD)

The UNSD acts as the central hub of the global statistical system. Its primary responsibilities are: 1. Global Coordination: Directing global statistical endeavors and ensuring that definitions and concepts are standardized so that data from India can be accurately compared to data from Brazil, the US, or Japan. 2. Data Repository: Serving as the central global repository for gathering, analyzing, and publishing statistics across every field for domestic and international use.

1.1.2.2 UN Regional Commissions

To facilitate cooperation among countries in specific geographic regions, the UN operates Regional Commissions. They implement global standards locally and offer technical support to participating governments.

  • India is a prominent member of the Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific (ESCAP).
  • Other commissions include the ECE (Europe), ECA (Africa), ECLAC (Latin America/Caribbean), and ESCWA (Western Asia).

1.1.2.3 Specialized International Agencies

The international system delegates specific statistical domains to specialized, independent agencies. These agencies set the ultimate global standards for their respective fields:

  • International Labour Organization (ILO): Sets standards for labour, employment, and wage statistics.
  • Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO): Manages global statistics regarding agriculture, forestry, and fisheries.
  • International Monetary Fund (IMF) & World Bank: Handle international financial statistics, balance of payments, and global economic development data.
  • World Health Organization (WHO): Standardizes health and mortality statistics (such as the International Classification of Diseases).
  • UNESCO: Manages global education and cultural statistics.

1.1.2.4 The UN Fundamental Principles of Official Statistics (UNFPOS)

To ensure that governments around the world produce statistics that citizens and the international community can trust, the UN established the UNFPOS. India formally adopted these principles in 2016. The core tenets include:

  • Relevance, Impartiality, and Equal Access: Official statistics are a public good and must be disseminated objectively and impartially.
  • Professionalism and Scientific Principles: Methods for data collection and processing must be strictly based on scientific principles and professional ethics, preventing political manipulation.
  • Confidentiality: Individual data collected for statistical purposes (whether from a person or a business) must be kept strictly confidential and used exclusively for statistical analysis.
  • Transparency: Statistical agencies must make their sources, methodologies, and procedures transparent to the public.

1.2 Methods of Collection of Official Statistics

The collection of official statistics involves structured methodologies designed to gather accurate, comprehensive, and reliable data about a country’s population, economy, and social conditions. Statistical systems generally rely on the following primary approaches:

1.2.1 Full Field Enumeration (The Traditional Census)

The traditional census approach comprises a comprehensive operation of actively collecting information from individuals and households on a range of topics at a specified time. It involves canvassing the entire country, reaching every single household, and collecting individual information within a brief, stipulated period.

  • Essential Features: This method is characterized by individual enumeration (each person is recorded separately), universality (covers the precisely defined national territory completely), simultaneity (data refers to a specific, well-defined point in time, known as the census moment), and defined periodicity (usually conducted decennially).
  • Advantages: It provides an unparalleled benchmark snapshot of the entire population at a specific period. It is uniquely capable of generating detailed data for the lowest geographic and administrative domains (small-area statistics), which is critical for local-level planning and the delimitation of electoral boundaries.
  • Limitations: It is the most elaborate, complex, and costly data collection activity a national statistical office undertakes. Because of the massive financial and administrative resources required, it is typically conducted only once every ten years. Consequently, the data tends to become outdated during the intercensal years.

1.2.2 Large-Scale Sample Surveys

Because of the rapid changes in socio-economic characteristics and the high cost of full censuses, statistical systems rely on continuing programmes of intercensal household sample surveys.

  • Methodology: Information is collected from a scientifically selected, representative subset of the population (the sample) rather than the entire population. The population census typically serves as the primary sampling frame for these surveys.
  • Advantages: Sample surveys are highly cost-effective and allow for the collection of detailed data on complex topics that are not suitable for a full census, such as labour force participation, consumer expenditure, poverty, and detailed industrial activities.
  • Limitations: Estimates derived from sample surveys are subject to sampling errors. Furthermore, because they rely on a smaller fraction of the population, they rarely provide reliable estimates for very small geographic areas or rare sub-populations.

1.2.3 The Register-Based Approach (Administrative Data)

In recent decades, there has been a significant shift toward utilizing existing administrative sources to produce official statistics.

  • Methodology: This approach extracts data from administrative registers that are primarily maintained for government operations, such as civil registration (births and deaths), tax records, employment registers, and education enrolment. To effectively compile census-like statistics, these individual registers are linked at the unit level, which typically requires the existence of a universal personal identification number for accurate record linkage.
  • Advantages: It significantly reduces the long-term costs of data collection and completely eliminates the response burden on the public. It also allows for the generation of statistical data at a much higher frequency (e.g., annually) compared to traditional censuses.
  • Limitations: The definitions, concepts, and reference periods in administrative registers are determined by administrative rules, which may not align with international statistical standards. Furthermore, individuals outside the formal administrative system (e.g., undocumented migrants or homeless populations) may be entirely excluded, leading to coverage errors.

1.2.4 Combined Census Methodologies

To optimize resources and data quality, many national statistical organizations use a combined approach, integrating administrative registers with field enumeration or sample surveys.

  • Methodology: Data from existing administrative registers are used directly as a source for certain statistical variables. For variables missing in the registers, or to verify existing data, the statistical office conducts ad-hoc sample surveys or partial field enumerations. In some instances, the statistical office will use register data to pre-fill census questionnaires, and the enumerator or respondent only needs to verify the information and answer any remaining questions.
  • Advantages: This hybrid approach reduces the length of the questionnaire and the burden on respondents while ensuring high-quality, comprehensive data collection.

1.2.5 The Rolling Census Approach

A rolling census represents a departure from the traditional model by conducting a continuous, cumulative survey that covers the whole country over a prolonged period (generally years), rather than a single short period of enumeration.

  • Methodology: A fraction of the population is sampled and surveyed each year. The data collected over multiple years (e.g., a five-year cycle) are then statistically cumulated and modelled to produce annual population estimates and small-area statistics.
  • Advantages: This approach smooths out the massive financial and labour peaks required for a traditional census and provides more frequently updated data.
  • Limitations: It requires highly complex sampling frames, continuous tracking of addresses, and sophisticated mathematical modelling techniques to estimate the data accurately across time periods.

1.2.6 Modern Data Capture Techniques

The collection of official statistics now utilizes multi-mode approaches and advanced technology to improve accuracy and efficiency.

  • Computer-Assisted Interviewing: Traditional paper questionnaires are being replaced by Computer-Assisted Personal Interviewing (CAPI) on portable devices (tablets/smartphones), Computer-Assisted Telephone Interviewing (CATI), and Internet-based self-enumeration. These systems have built-in logic controls to automatically check for consistency, skip irrelevant questions, and prevent missing values during the interview.
  • Optical Reading Technology: For paper-based data collection, statistical offices use Optical Mark Reading (OMR), Optical Character Reading (OCR), and Intelligent Character Recognition (ICR) to digitally scan and convert handwritten responses directly into computer data, drastically reducing manual data entry errors.
  • Geospatial Technology: Data collection is increasingly supported by Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS). Enumerators use these to capture the exact geographic coordinates of households to ensure complete coverage and prevent duplication.
